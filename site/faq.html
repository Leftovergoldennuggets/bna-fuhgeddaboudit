Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to common questions about this project and the data behind it. For a more detailed technical discussion, see our Methodology page.

Where does this data come from?

Two sources: NHTSA (the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration) and Waymo's Safety Impact Data Hub.

NHTSA requires all autonomous vehicle companies to report every crash publicly through their Standing General Order program. Waymo publishes a curated dataset on their own website that adds crash type classifications and other context. We merge both datasets to create the most complete picture available.

What time period does this cover?

The dataset covers September 2020 through September 2025 — five years of Waymo crash reports. It includes 1,123 total reported incidents across five U.S. cities.

How often is this website updated?

The site is designed to update automatically every quarter. NHTSA data is continuously available, and Waymo updates their Safety Impact Data Hub approximately every three months (around March, June, September, and December). Our automated pipeline re-runs whenever new data is published.

What counts as a "crash"?

Under NHTSA's Standing General Order, companies must report any incident involving an autonomous vehicle that results in property damage, injury, or the deployment of an airbag. This includes very minor incidents — like being bumped by another car in a parking lot — that would normally never make the news.

This low reporting threshold means the crash numbers look high, but the vast majority of reported incidents are minor property-damage-only events with no injuries.

How serious are these crashes?

The vast majority are minor — property damage only, with no injuries. Out of 1,123 reported crashes:

  • 96 had any injury reported at all
  • 15 involved moderate, serious, or fatal injuries
  • 1 involved a fatality (a secondary crash where a human-driven vehicle struck the Waymo)

On the map, crashes are color-coded by severity: grey for no injury, amber for any injury, and red for serious incidents. You can click the red markers during the "Most Serious Incidents" section to read the full NHTSA narratives.

How does Waymo compare to human drivers?

According to Waymo's peer-reviewed research (published in Traffic Injury Prevention), their autonomous vehicles had 90% fewer serious crashes and 81% fewer injury-causing crashes compared to human driver benchmarks over 56.7 million rider-only miles.

However, these comparisons come with important caveats: the study was authored by Waymo researchers, and their vehicles operate in specific conditions (certain cities, mostly good weather, urban environments) that may not perfectly match the "average" human driving experience.

Are the crash locations on the map exact?

Most are accurate to the street level. NHTSA redacts GPS coordinates but provides street addresses for nearly all crashes. We geocode these addresses using OpenStreetMap's Nominatim service, achieving a 92% success rate (1,026 out of 1,119 mapped crashes placed at their real street location).

The remaining 8% of markers use an approximate city-center location and are flagged as "approximate location" in their popups. Accuracy varies by city — San Francisco (98%) and Los Angeles (98%) have the highest rates, while Phoenix (77%) is lower due to less detailed address descriptions in some reports.

In which cities does Waymo operate?

Based on the crash data, Waymo has reported incidents in five major cities:

  • San Francisco — 527 crashes (46.9%)
  • Phoenix — 312 crashes (27.8%)
  • Los Angeles — 208 crashes (18.5%)
  • Austin — 61 crashes (5.4%)
  • Atlanta — 13 crashes (1.2%)

San Francisco has the most crashes because Waymo has operated there the longest and runs the most vehicles. A small number of crashes (2) occurred in Mountain View near Waymo's headquarters.

What are the most common crash types?

The most common crash type is rear-end collisions — where another vehicle hits the back of the Waymo. This is also the most common crash type for human drivers. Other frequent types include side-impact collisions and intersection crashes.

Crashes involving pedestrians, cyclists, or motorcyclists are less common but receive special attention because of the higher potential for injury. You can filter by these categories in the Explore section.

Why do most crashes happen during rush hour?

About 31% of crashes occur during rush hours (7–9 AM and 5–7 PM). This isn't surprising — these are the times when roads are busiest and the most vehicles (both human-driven and autonomous) are on the road. More traffic means more opportunities for collisions.

Can I download the raw data?

Yes! The original data is publicly available:

  • NHTSA Standing General Order Data
  • Waymo Safety Impact Data Hub

Our code and processing pipeline are also open-source, so you can reproduce the entire analysis.
