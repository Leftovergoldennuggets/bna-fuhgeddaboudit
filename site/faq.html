Frequently Asked Questions

Quick answers to common questions about this project and the data behind it. For a more detailed technical discussion, see our Methodology page.

Where does this data come from?

Two sources: NHTSA (the National Highway Traffic Safety Administration) and Waymo's Safety Impact Data Hub.

NHTSA requires all autonomous vehicle companies to report crashes resulting in injury, airbag deployment, or property damage exceeding $1,000 through their Standing General Order program. Waymo publishes a curated dataset on their own website that adds crash type classifications and other context. We merge both datasets to create the most complete picture available.

What time period does this cover?

The dataset covers -- through --. However, the data is heavily concentrated in 2023–2025, reflecting Waymo's expansion from early commercial operations to large-scale service.

In the earliest years of the dataset (2020–2022), only 9 crashes were reported total — Waymo's fleet was small and still operating with safety drivers in many cases. The data becomes substantial starting in 2023 when Waymo launched fully public, rider-only service in San Francisco, and accelerates through 2024 and 2025 as the company expanded to Phoenix, Los Angeles, Austin, and Atlanta.

All crashes in this dataset involve fully autonomous driving — the Waymo vehicle was operating without a human driver (rider-only mode) at the time of the incident.

How often is this website updated?

The site is designed to update automatically every quarter. NHTSA data is continuously available, and Waymo updates their Safety Impact Data Hub approximately every three months (around March, June, September, and December). Our automated pipeline re-runs whenever new data is published.

Did the reporting rules change in 2025?

Yes. On June 16, 2025, NHTSA's Third Amended Standing General Order took effect, changing the crash reporting requirements for autonomous vehicle companies. Key changes include:

  • A property damage threshold was added — companies no longer need to report very minor property-damage-only incidents below a certain level
  • Reporting deadlines were extended from one day to five days for the most serious crashes
  • Duplicate reporting by multiple companies involved in the same crash was eliminated

This means data from before and after June 2025 isn't perfectly comparable — the earlier data includes some very minor incidents that wouldn't meet the new reporting threshold. Our analysis covers both periods and notes this distinction in the Methodology page.

What counts as a "crash"?

Under NHTSA's Standing General Order, companies must report any incident involving an autonomous vehicle that results in injury, airbag deployment, or property damage exceeding $1,000. Before June 2025, the threshold was even lower — any property damage at all was reportable, which included very minor incidents like parking lot bumps.

This relatively low reporting threshold means the crash numbers look high, but the vast majority of reported incidents are minor property-damage-only events with no injuries.

What was Waymo doing when these crashes happened?

In nearly two-thirds of reported crashes (65.6%), the Waymo vehicle was traveling at 0 mph — completely stopped — at the time of contact. The median pre-crash speed across all incidents is 0 mph, and the mean is just 4.1 mph.

The most common scenario is a stationary Waymo being hit by another vehicle that is backing up, changing lanes, or proceeding through an intersection. The most frequent crash types are rear-end collisions (26%), side-impact collisions (21%), and backing collisions (15%).

This doesn't mean the other driver is always at fault — crash reports describe circumstances, not legal responsibility. But it does mean that most reported "crashes" look very different from what the word typically implies.

How does Waymo compare to human drivers?

Waymo publishes crash rate comparisons on its Safety Impact Data Hub. Using a methodology peer-reviewed in the journal Traffic Injury Prevention (Kusano et al., 2025), Waymo reports that over 127 million rider-only miles, its vehicles had 90% fewer serious crashes and 81% fewer injury-causing crashes compared to adjusted human driver benchmarks.

Important caveats:

  • These comparisons were conducted by Waymo researchers, though the methodology has been peer-reviewed by independent academics
  • The human benchmarks are adjusted to reflect the specific areas where Waymo operates (urban surface streets), using a "dynamic benchmarking" method. This means the comparison isn't against the average American driver everywhere — it's against human drivers in similar conditions
  • The methodology adheres to the RAVE checklist (Retrospective Automated Vehicle Evaluation), an industry standard developed jointly by researchers, academics, and insurance organizations including the Insurance Institute for Highway Safety

We include these figures for context but note that they are Waymo's own analysis of their own performance.

What are the most common crash types?

The most common crash type is rear-end collisions — where another vehicle hits the back of the Waymo. This is also the most common crash type for human drivers. Other frequent types include side-impact collisions and intersection crashes.

Crashes involving pedestrians, cyclists, or motorcyclists are less common but receive special attention because of the higher potential for injury. You can filter by these categories in the Explore section.

Does crash type mean Waymo was at fault?

No. Crash types (e.g., "rear-end collision") describe the physical dynamics of the collision — where contact occurred between vehicles. They do not indicate legal fault. NHTSA reports do not assign fault or determine responsibility. In most crashes in this dataset, the Waymo vehicle was stationary at the time of contact.

Why do most crashes happen during rush hour?

About --% of crashes occur during rush hours (7–9 AM and 5–7 PM). This isn't surprising — these are the times when roads are busiest and the most vehicles (both human-driven and autonomous) are on the road. More traffic means more opportunities for collisions.

Why are there so many crashes in parking lots?

About 28% of reported crashes occur in parking areas. This is consistent with industry research showing that parking lots account for a significant share of all vehicle collisions — the tight spaces, frequent reversing, and pedestrian traffic create more opportunities for minor contact.

Nearly all parking lot crashes in the dataset resulted in property damage only, with no injuries reported.
